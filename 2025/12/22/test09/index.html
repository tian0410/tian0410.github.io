Trying to make an autistic child “become normal”—why is that doomed to be a dead end?

Created2025-12-22|Updated2025-12-22|/Experience and Sharing
|Post View:

The reason autism makes it hard to survive in the world isn’t, at its core, that their way of thinking and behaving is unusual. It’s that they never truly get to develop a way of thinking that belongs to them.

From a young age, they’re subjected to a large amount of intervention and conditioning, with the goal of becoming—or getting close to—“ordinary people,” rather than understanding themselves and becoming themselves.

As a result, they can’t consistently use their own thinking to make sense of the world, and they can’t form ideas that are truly their own.

A person without their own thoughts is just a puppet on strings. Of course they can’t live independently.

When parents and society don’t accept a child’s “oddness,” the child can’t form an independent self, and independence becomes impossible.

So any attempt to “train” and “intervene” to make a child “normal like everyone else” is destined to be a dead end.

What autistic children truly need isn’t to “become normal,” but to “become at ease”—able to use their own senses to feel freely, think freely, and act freely.

Parents should do their best to ensure safety and health, and create an environment where the child can feel at ease. Only then can the child use their own self to experience and understand the world.

By preserving their neurodiversity—and making use of those diverse traits—they can ultimately live independently.
