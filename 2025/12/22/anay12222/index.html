Hamp messes around in the kitchen cooking all day, but Chengguo never goes into the kitchen

Created2025-12-22|Updated2025-12-22|/Experience and Sharing
|Post View:

（The following is an article by Cheng Guo’s mother）
Chengguo turns eighteen this year. From the age of two until seven, I put him through on-and-off interventions. Even though I doubted those interventions in my mind, in action I kept going. Because when a family lives together, it’s either you intervene with me or I intervene with you—life doesn’t stop.

I remember when he was five or six: he could even fry an egg himself. He went into the kitchen wanting to cook something, and I taught him how to pour oil. After that, he never went into the kitchen again.

Later, it became spoon-feeding him, because letting food get cold is bad for the stomach, and reheating it again and again is troublesome. I also wanted him to finish sooner so I could wash the dishes. And the way he struggled with eating wasn’t “proper,” and it bothered me to watch. The principle of intervention is that rules come first, people come second. You treat eating as a task to be completed and ignore the child’s feelings in their body and stomach.

From around the time he was seven or eight, I began learning to align my thoughts and actions, but the lingering afterheat of intervention never fully went away. Around age seven, the aftereffects of intervention started to show up little by little. His emotions began having problems—he would cry easily, feeling wronged. At amusement parks, if other kids wanted to play with him, he would hit or shove them, no matter how young or old they were; they couldn’t even get close.

After he turned ten, the aggressive behavior worsened—for example, he hit a security screener because the screener told him to take his bag off for inspection, and wouldn’t let him through otherwise. He would also gouge at other people’s eyeballs—suddenly, with no warning. There was no cause at the time, and I wasn’t on guard.

Even after letting go for more than ten years, the hitting still happens occasionally—maybe once or twice a year.

Around 1:00 a.m. on August 14, 2025, Chengguo ran over to Grandma’s place across the way to eat. For him, this was a historic breakthrough, and I want to record this moment. Some kids—if you stop them once—they might retreat for two years, or five years, or ten years. If you say something about what they’re doing, they’ll stop doing it. Chengguo is the kind of “autistic kid” who easily retreats.

Still in his staying-up-late phase, Chengguo didn’t sleep at all that night, and didn’t nap during the day either. He ate breakfast and lunch, and in the afternoon he went to the shop with his dad. A little after 6 p.m., he got home and fell asleep without eating dinner. He woke up after 10 p.m., and around 1 a.m.—probably hungry—he went to Grandma’s place across the hall to eat.

He even dug out a pot that hadn’t been used in a long time, hooked it up with water, and poured who-knows-what into it. That action is super simple for us, but extremely hard for Chengguo—and in ten years, he had never done anything like that.

Later we asked him what was in the pot, and he said, “Forget it, don’t ask,” so we shut up. Grandma said that the paper she used to wipe the range hood fell into the congee pot, so she poured the congee out. Chengguo saw there was no congee left to drink, so he wanted to make some himself.

Grandma said that when she heard noise in the kitchen and realized it was Chengguo, she didn’t say anything and didn’t come out—because I had told her to try not to interfere and to let go as much as possible. Grandma also knows that if she comes out, Chengguo will let her do everything.

Hamp messes around in the kitchen cooking all day, but Chengguo never goes into the kitchen. Still, with all those years of cooking photos as a foundation, it’s not that Chengguo can’t cook—he just doesn’t want to. Cooking skills are never the real issue; what’s needed is the emotional energy, the will to want to cook. But once he’s taken this first step, things should move much faster from here on.
